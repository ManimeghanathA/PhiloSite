Socrates

Socrates was a foundational figure in Western philosophy, known for his method of questioning to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate ideas. His approach, called the Socratic method, emphasized dialogue and the pursuit of virtue and knowledge. Socrates never wrote down his teachings, and most of what we know comes from his student, Plato.

Plato

Plato, a student of Socrates, was a prolific philosopher who founded the Academy in Athens, one of the first institutions of higher learning in the Western world. His works explore ideal forms of government, justice, and the nature of reality, with his theory of forms suggesting that the material world is only a shadow of more perfect, abstract realities.

Aristotle

Aristotle, a student of Plato, made significant contributions to many fields, including logic, ethics, biology, and metaphysics. His development of syllogistic logic and his categorization of knowledge laid the foundation for scientific methodology. Unlike Plato, Aristotle believed in the importance of empirical observation and practical experience.

René Descartes

René Descartes is often referred to as the father of modern philosophy. A French thinker and mathematician, he sought to establish a foundation for knowledge based on doubt and skepticism. His famous declaration, "Cogito, ergo sum" ("I think, therefore I am"), encapsulates his belief in the primacy of reason in the search for truth.

Heraclitus

Heraclitus was an ancient Greek philosopher best known for his doctrine of change, captured in his statement, "You cannot step into the same river twice." He believed that the universe is in a constant state of flux and that fire is the fundamental substance underlying all change, reflecting his view of life’s eternal transformation.
